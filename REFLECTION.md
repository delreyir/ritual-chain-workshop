# Reflection

> **What should be public, what should stay hidden, and what should be decided by
> AI versus by a human in a bounty system?**

The rules of the game should be fully public: the title, the rubric, the reward,
the deadlines, and the list of participating addresses, because a bounty is only
fair if everyone can see and trust the conditions before they enter. The content
of each answer should stay hidden during the submission window, since the moment
answers are visible, latecomers can copy and out-improve earlier entries, which
is exactly the flaw this project fixes with commitments and TEE encryption. Once
judging is complete, the answers (or at least an integrity proof of them) can
become public, so the outcome is auditable and the community can check that the
judging was reasonable. The AI is best used for the subjective, high-volume work
of reading every answer against the rubric and producing a comparative ranking,
which it can do consistently and without favoritism. The deterministic rules,
however, should be decided by the contract, not the AI: deadlines, commitment
validity, one-submission-per-address, and a single payout are objective
constraints that must hold even if the model misbehaves. The final winner
selection and the release of funds should stay with a human owner who treats the
AI ranking as a strong recommendation, not a binding verdict, because money is
irreversible and accountability for it should rest with a person. In short:
publish the rules, hide the answers until judging, let AI score and the contract
enforce, and keep the human in the loop for the irreversible final decision.
